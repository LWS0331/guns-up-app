## 1. Long-term athletic development (LTAD) frameworks

Balyi's 7-stage LTAD model (Balyi, Way & Higgs, 2013) was the field's
foundational document but is now considered empirically weak. Stages: Active
Start (0–6); FUNdamentals (boys 6–9 / girls 6–8); Learn to Train (boys 9–12 /
girls 8–11) — Balyi's claimed "golden age of motor learning"; Train to Train
(boys 12–16 / girls 11–15); Train to Compete (boys 16–23 / girls 15–21); Train
to Win (boys 19+ / girls 18+); Active for Life.

Ford et al. (2011, *J Sports Sci* 29(4):389–402) delivered the most influential
critique: the model is one-dimensional (essentially physiological), the "windows
of opportunity" claims derive from cross-sectional Soviet-era data, and it lacks
individualization. Van Hooren & De Ste Croix (2020, *Strength Cond J*
42(6):7–14): "general motor abilities and the associated sensitive periods do
not exist… they should not be used to train youth athletes."

**Canada Soccer's "Wellness to World Cup"** (CSA, 2008/2009) operationalizes
Balyi for soccer with training-to-competition ratios sliding from ~70:30 in
Learn to Train through ~40:60 in Train to Compete and as high as 5:12
(training:competition) at Train to Win.

**US Soccer Player Development Initiatives (PDI, mandatory August 2017):**
- Birth-year (calendar) registration
- Build-out lines for U9/U10 (no offside between midfield and the line;
  goalkeepers must roll/throw/pass — no punts/drop-kicks; an indirect free kick
  punishes violations)
- Small-sided standards: U6/U8 4v4 no GK, ball size 3, 25–35×15–25 yds; U9/U10
  7v7 with GK, ball size 4, 47×30 yds; U11/U12 9v9, ball size 4, 70×45 yds;
  U13+ 11v11, size 5
- **Heading restrictions:** no heading U11 and below; U12–U13 limited to ≤30
  minutes/week and ≤15–20 headers/player/week in practice; no in-match
  restrictions

**English FA "England DNA"** (FA, 2014): Foundation Phase (U5–U11; "love the
ball, master the ball" with ≥70% ball-rolling time), Youth Development Phase
(U12–U16), Professional Development Phase (U17–U21), all governed by the
4-corner model (Technical/Tactical, Physical, Psychological, Social — endorsed
by Mallows et al. 2024, *J Sports Sci* 42(2)).

**German DFB post-Euro-2000 reform** invested €48M+/year, mandated youth
academies (Leistungszentren) at all 36 Bundesliga 1/2 clubs, and built 366
Stützpunkte scouting ~600,000 youth players annually — producing the 2014
World Cup–winning generation (Güllich, 2014, *Eur J Sport Sci* 14(6):530–537).

**Youth Physical Development (YPD) model — Lloyd & Oliver (2012, *Strength
Cond J* 34(3):61–72)** is the peer-reviewed alternative the AI grounds in.
Core thesis: all fitness components are trainable across childhood and
adolescence; the magnitude and mechanism of adaptation varies with maturation
(neural pre-PHV; combined neural + structural during/post-PHV). NSCA Position
Statement on Long-Term Athletic Development (Lloyd, Cronin, Faigenbaum et al.,
2016, *J Strength Cond Res* 30(6):1491–1509) codifies the 10 Pillars,
including the explicit claim that "muscular strength is the underpinning
quality" of youth development.

### Biological vs. chronological age

Single most operationally important distinction.

- **Mirwald et al. (2002, *MSSE* 34(4):689–694)** equation predicts maturity
  offset (years before/after PHV) from age, height, sitting height, leg length,
  and weight; standard error ~0.59 years; most accurate within ±1 year of PHV.
- **Khamis-Roche (1994, *Pediatrics* 94(4):504–507)** predicts adult stature
  using current measurements + mid-parent stature; bio-banding groups players
  at 85–90%, 90–95%, >95% predicted adult stature.
- Mean PHV: **boys ~14.0 years (range 12–16); girls ~12.0 years (range
  10–14)** — within a same-age cohort, biological age can vary by 4–5 years.

### Relative Age Effect (RAE)

Robust across European and US academy soccer: Q1 ≈ 40–45%, Q4 ≈ 9–12%
(Helsen et al. 2005, *J Sports Sci* 23(6):629–636; replicated in Helsen et
al. 2012). Mitigation: bio-banding (Cumming et al. 2017), age-ordered shirt
numbering (Mann & van Ginneken 2017 — effective; just providing birthdates
is not), quotas, and longitudinal selection rounds.

### Sampling vs. specialization

Côté's Developmental Model of Sport Participation recommends multi-sport
sampling 6–12, specializing 13–15, investment 16+. Soccer is a "late-peak"
sport.

## 2. Age-appropriate physical training

### Youth resistance training is safe and beneficial when properly designed

NSCA Position Statement (Faigenbaum, Kraemer, Blimkie, Jeffreys, Micheli,
Nitka & Rowland, 2009, *JSCR* 23(5 Suppl):S60–S79):
> "A properly designed and supervised resistance training program is relatively
> safe for youth" and "can increase a young athlete's resistance to
> sports-related injuries."

Default prescription: 2–3 nonconsecutive days/week; 1–3 sets × 6–15 reps for
strength exercises; 1–3 sets × 3–6 reps for power; 5–10 min dynamic warm-up;
5–10% load increases as form improves.

The 2014 International Consensus (Lloyd, Faigenbaum et al., *BJSM*
48(7):498–505) confirms growth-plate concerns are not supported by scientific
evidence; reported youth strength training injury rates (~0.001% per
participation hour) are far below those of soccer itself.

**The "stunts growth" myth is decisively debunked.** Faigenbaum 2009, the
2014 International Consensus, and Malina (2006, *CJSM* 16:478): "a growth-plate
fracture has not been reported in any youth resistance training study."

### Stage-progressed prescription

| Stage | Age | Prescription |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-PHV | U10–U12 | Body weight, medicine ball (1–2 kg), bands, child-sized DBs; 1–2 sets × 10–15 reps; RPE 3–6/10; 2–3 d/wk; 30–45 min sessions; pattern mastery (squat, hinge, push, pull, lunge, brace, carry, rotate); strength gains predominantly neural |
| Peri-PHV | U13–U14 | Loaded compounds with light loads (≤50% est. 1RM); 2–3 sets × 8–12 reps; RPE 5–7/10; vigilance for Osgood-Schlatter, Sever's, SLJS during rapid limb growth ("adolescent awkwardness," Quatman-Yates et al. 2012) |
| Peri/post-PHV | U15–U16 | 65–85% 1RM; 3–4 sets × 5–10 reps; structured periodization; introduce Olympic lift derivatives |
| Post-PHV | U17–U18 | Adult-style programming; 70–92% 1RM across periodized blocks |

### Plyometric volume guidelines

Synthesized from Bedoya et al. 2015, Lloyd et al. plyometric studies, and
NSCA Essentials.

| Age | Contacts/session | Frequency | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| U10–U12 | 50–80 | 1–2×/wk | Low-intensity bilateral |
| U13–U15 | 80–120 | 2×/wk | ≥72 h between sessions; introduce unilateral |
| U16–U18 | 120–200 | 2–3×/wk | Depth jumps reserved for post-PHV with squat ≥1× BW |

Surface: grass, rubber, or sprung floor — never concrete for high-intensity
reactive work.

### Speed development

Rumpf et al. 2012; Oliver, Lloyd & Rumpf 2013. >85% of soccer match sprints
are <20 m, so acceleration dominates.

| Age | Distance | Reps |
|---|---|---|
| U10–U12 | 5–10 m | 6–10 |
| U13–U15 | 10–30 m | 8–12 |
| U16–U18 | 10–50 m | 8–14 |

Always 1:20–1:60 work:rest, terminating when times deviate >5% from best.

### Agility

Sheppard & Young (2006, *J Sports Sci* 24(9):919–932) distinguish
pre-planned change-of-direction (CODS) from reactive open-skill agility. Pre-PHV
is the "neural window" for movement competency; ≥50% of agility work for
adolescents should be reactive/open-skill.

### Aerobic capacity

Buchheit & Laursen (2013, *Sports Med* 43:313/927) HIIT framework — long
intervals (4×4 min @ 90–95% HRmax), short intervals (15s/15s, 30s/30s @
100–110% vVO₂max), repeated sprints, and SSGs. Long, slow distance for
"conditioning" pre-PHV is discredited — lacks specificity, raises overuse
risk, correlates with sport dropout.

### Anaerobic capacity

Suppressed pre-PHV (Falk & Dotan 2006). Children rely more on aerobic
pathways for short, intense work. Structured lactate-tolerance work should
generally be deferred to U15+/post-PHV. Bishop, Girard & Mendez-Villanueva
(2011, *Sports Med* 41:673/741) RSA framework — improving single-sprint speed
and recovery between sprints — is the appropriate target.

### Mobility / FMS

Bonazza et al. (2017, *AJSM* 45:725) found composite FMS ≤14 elevated injury
OR to 2.74, but Moran et al. (2017, *BJSM* 51:1661) concluded FMS "does not
support its use as an injury prediction tool." Use as screening + corrective,
not predictive cutoff.

### Static stretching pre-match is a known performance impairment

Behm & Chaouachi (2011, *Eur J Appl Physiol* 111:2633): >60-second holds
acutely reduce strength/sprint/jump 4–8%. Replace with dynamic warm-up +
FIFA 11+ / 11+ Kids.

## 3. Soccer-specific performance

### Position-specific match demands (adult)

| Position | TD | High-Intensity (>19.8 km/h) | Sprint (>25.2 km/h) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Goalkeeper | ~5.5 km | ~56 m | ~11 m (~10 high-speed actions) |
| Centre Back | 9.5–10.5 km | 600–800 m | 150–250 m |
| Full Back | 10.5–11.2 km | 1000–1200 m | 250–350 m |
| Central Midfielder | 11.5–13.0 km (highest) | 900–1100 m | 150–250 m |
| Winger | 11.0–11.8 km | 1100–1300 m (highest) | 350–450 m (highest) |
| Striker | 10.5–11.0 km | 800–1000 m | 250–350 m |

### Youth-scaled estimates (Buchheit et al. 2010)

| Age | Total Distance | High-Speed Running |
|---|---|---|
| U12 (9v9, 2×30 min) | 4.5–5.5 km | 250–400 m |
| U14 | 6.5–8.0 km | 500–800 m |
| U15–U16 | 8.0–10.0 km | 800–1100 m |
| U17–U18 | 9.5–11.5 km | 1000–1400 m |

Within-age maturation is decisive: more-mature U15s cover significantly more
distance >16 km/h.

### Technical-tactical curriculum by age

- **U10–U12:** 1v1 ball-mastery (Coerver-style, La Masia rondos at 4v2/5v2 on
  8×8 yds), basic passing/receiving, 1000+ touches per session, no structured
  tactics
- **U13–U14:** speed of play under pressure, scanning every 2–3 s, finishing
  variety, pressing-trigger introduction
- **U15–U16:** position-specific refinement, full tactical concepts
  (transitions, blocks, set pieces)
- **U17–U18:** pre-elite execution under pressure and fatigue

### Small-sided games (SSGs)

Hill-Haas, Dawson, Impellizzeri & Coutts (2011, *Sports Med* 41(3):199–220):
smaller formats (3v3, 4v4) on ~75–200 m²/player elicit ~80–90% HRmax,
equivalent to match-play, while delivering 5–7× more touches per player than
11v11. Larger formats (8v8, 9v9) build tactical patterns and shape.

### Soccer-specific conditioning

Reflects intermittent profile (~150–250 high-intensity bouts, sprints averaging
2 s, recovery 45–90 s — Mohr 2003). 30-15 IFT (Buchheit 2008) and Yo-Yo IR1/IR2
(Bangsbo, Iaia & Krustrup 2008) are the validated youth-applicable tests. RSA
prescription (Bishop): 6×40 m shuttle sprints with 180° turn, 20 s passive
recovery, 2–4 sets.

### Set pieces / GK specialization

No structured set-piece work U10–U12; introduce at U13+. Goalkeeper
specialization should not occur before U13–U14; pre-13 GKs benefit from
outfield play to develop foot skills and game understanding.

## 4. Injury prevention (highest-stakes section)

### FIFA 11+ Kids (ages 7–13)

F-MARC's 7-exercise, 5-level, 15–20-min warm-up that uniquely includes a
fall/roll-over technique. **Rössler et al. (2018, *Sports Med* 48(6):1493–1504)
multinational cluster RCT:**
- ~48% reduction in overall injuries
- ~74% reduction in severe injuries
- ~55% reduction in lower-extremity injuries

Delivered ≥1×/week (dose-response favoring 2×/week). Beaudouin et al. 2019
confirmed the severe-injury reduction; Rössler et al. 2019 (*BJSM*
53(5):309–314) documented a >50% reduction in healthcare costs.

### FIFA 11+ (ages 14+)

15 exercises in 3 parts (running ~8 min, strength/balance/plyometrics ~10 min,
advanced running ~2 min); on match days, only Parts 1 and 3 are performed.

Soligard et al. (2008, *BMJ* 337:a2469) cluster RCT (1,892 Norwegian female
13–17 yr): overall injury rate ~32% lower (RR 0.68); severe-injury risk ~45%
lower; overuse-injury risk ~53% lower. Silvers-Granelli et al. (2015, *AJSM*
43(11):2628–2637): 46.1% overall reduction; 77% reduction in ACL injuries in
compliant collegiate male teams.

**Compliance is the single strongest predictor of effect.**

### ACL injury prevention

Critical because female soccer players have **3–8× higher non-contact ACL
injury rates than males** (Hewett et al. 2005, *AJSM* 33(4):492–501).

- **PEP Program** (Mandelbaum et al. 2005, *AJSM* 33(7):1003–1010): 88%
  reduction year 1, 74% year 2 in 14–18-yr club soccer; Gilchrist et al. 2008
  NCAA RCT: 3.3× lower non-contact ACL rate
- **Knäkontroll/Knee Control** (Waldén et al. 2012, *BMJ* 344:e3042): 64%
  reduction in ACL injury overall; 88% reduction in compliant players
- **Sportsmetrics** (Hewett et al. 1999, *AJSM* 27(6):699–706): 72% reduction
  in non-contact knee injuries

> **Knowledge boundary:** ACL rehab and return-to-play decisions belong to
> orthopedic surgeons, PTs, and athletic trainers — never the AI/coach.

### Growth-related conditions (recognize, modify, refer)

| Condition | Age | Sign | Management | Catastrophic flag |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sever's (calcaneal apophysitis) | 8–15 | Pain on mediolateral squeeze of posterior calcaneus | Relative rest, heel cups, calf stretching, ice. Self-limiting with apophyseal closure | — |
| Osgood-Schlatter | 10–15 | Tibial-tubercle pain with prominence | Pain-guided activity modification, PT, patellar straps | **AVULSION FRACTURE — refer immediately** for sudden severe pain or inability to extend the knee |
| Sinding-Larsen-Johansson | 10–14 | Inferior patellar pole | Same as Osgood-Schlatter | — |
| Iselin's | 8–13 | 5th metatarsal | Differentiate from Jones fracture — refer for X-ray | Jones fracture |
| Apophyseal avulsion (ASIS, AIIS, ischial tuberosity, lesser trochanter) | Peripubertal | Sudden pop, severe pain, inability to bear weight | **REFER IMMEDIATELY** | — |

**Salter-Harris classification** (Salter & Harris 1963, *JBJS* 45:587–622):
peripubertal joint trauma must be evaluated for physeal fracture; "ankle sprain
in a 12-year-old" can be a Salter-Harris fracture of the distal fibula —
**always X-ray.**

### Concussion — US Soccer 2015 Concussion Initiative

Settlement of *Mehr v. FIFA / U.S. Soccer*:

> "Players in 11-U programs and younger shall not engage in heading, either in
> practices or in games. Limited heading in practice for players in 12-U and
> 13-U programs… maximum of 30 minutes of heading training per week, with no
> more than 15–20 headers per player, per week."

Lipton et al. (2013, *Radiology* 268(3):850–857) found amateur adult players
heading >885–1,550 times/year had reduced fractional anisotropy in
temporal-occipital white matter; >1,800/year correlated with poorer memory.
Most soccer concussions arise from player-to-player contact during heading
attempts (Comstock et al. 2015, *JAMA Pediatr*) — supporting both heading
limits and aggressive enforcement of head-area fouls.

**Female adolescents have ~1.5–2× higher concussion rates** than males in
equivalent soccer (Marar et al. 2012, *AJSM* 40(4):747–755), with longer
recovery.

Concussion management follows the **5th International Consensus Statement**
(McCrory et al. 2017, *BJSM* 51(11):838–847; Berlin) and the **6th**
(Patricios et al. 2023, *BJSM*; Amsterdam). SCAT5/Child SCAT5/SCAT6 are
clinician tools; CRT5 enables non-medical recognize-and-remove.

> **Return-to-play is a MEDICAL DECISION ONLY — the AI/coach must never clear
> an athlete.**

### Specialization risk

Jayanthi et al. (2015, *AJSM* 43(4):794–801) defined specialization as: (1)
main sport chosen, (2) other sports quit, (3) >8 months/year. Highly
specialized athletes have independently elevated overuse-injury risk.

**AAP, AOSSM, NATA, AMSSM consensus thresholds:**
- Weekly hours ≤ chronological age
- ≤16 h/week total organized sport regardless of age
- >2:1 free-play : organized-practice pre-puberty
- ≥1–2 days off/week
- 2–3 months off/year (cumulative)
- Delay specialization to age ~15–16

### Recovery between matches

48–72 h minimum between competitive matches; tournament play with 3–4 matches
per weekend produces known increases in muscle/concussion risk; multiple games
per day are not recommended for youth.

### Female-specific RED-S/triad

Mountjoy et al. 2014/2018/2023 IOC consensus: warning signs are amenorrhea,
stress fractures, restrictive eating, weight loss, recurrent illness.

> Eating-disorder identification and treatment is **REFER ONLY** (RD with
> eating-disorder expertise + pediatrician + mental-health professional).

## 5. Strength & conditioning program design

### Periodization for youth soccer

- **Off-season (4–8 wk post-season):** GPP, hypertrophy/strength accumulation
  (3–4×8–12, 65–80% 1RM); aerobic base; technique re-skilling. 3 RT
  sessions/wk
- **Pre-season (4–6 wk):** specific strength + power, HIIT for VO₂max, RSA,
  plyometric loading
- **In-season:** maintenance with 1–2 RT sessions/wk; CNS-friendly low-volume
  high-intensity (1–2 sets × 4–6 reps, 80–90% 1RM)

### Linear vs. undulating vs. block

- Linear best for U13–U15 novices
- Undulating good for in-season (Kraemer & Fleck)
- Block periodization for U16–U18 academy/varsity with developed competency
- Adolescents respond particularly well to nonlinear programming (NSCA 2009)

### Concurrent training interference

Wilson, Marin, Rhea et al. (2012, *JSCR* 26:2293) meta-analysis: concurrent
training reduces strength/hypertrophy/power gains; running interference is
worse than cycling. Mitigations: separate sessions ≥6–9 h, lift before
running, limit endurance to ≤3×/wk of ≤30 min, prefer SSG/cycling. Importantly,
interference is reduced in untrained youth because both qualities are far from
ceilings (Marta et al. 2013; Chaouachi et al. 2014).

### Total weekly load caps (Jayanthi rule + practical translation)

| Age | Soccer | Match | S&C | Free play | TOTAL CAP |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| U10 | 2–3 h | 1 h in-practice | — | 5–10 h | <10 h |
| U12 | 3–4 h | 1–2 h | 1–1.5 h | 4–8 h | ≤12 h |
| U14 | 4–6 h | 1.5–2 h | 1.5–2 h | 3–6 h | ≤14 h |
| U16 | 6–8 h | 2 h | 2–3 h | 2–4 h | ≤16 h |
| U18 | 8–10 h | 2–2.5 h | 2.5–3 h | 1–3 h | ≤18 h |

### Recovery monitoring tools (in order of practicality for youth)

- **Borg CR-10 / Foster sRPE** (most practical) — RPE × min duration = AU;
  validated in youth soccer (Impellizzeri et al. 2004, *MSSE* 36:1042)
- **Hooper Index** daily 4-item wellness questionnaire (sleep, stress, fatigue,
  soreness, 1–7) — 60 seconds per athlete
- **CMJ for fatigue** (>5% drop = neuromuscular fatigue, Claudino et al. 2017)
- HRV is **questionable for U10–U15** due to autonomic immaturity
- ACWR (Gabbett 2016) — sweet spot 0.8–1.3 — but Dalen-Lorentsen et al. 2021
  RCT in 482 elite youth found NO injury-rate benefit; use as one signal, not
  deterministic

## 6. Nutrition for youth athletes

### Energy requirements

Per Desbrow et al. 2014 SDA position (*IJSNEM* 24(5):570–584); Thomas, Erdman
& Burke 2016 (*J Acad Nutr Diet* 116(3):501–528):

| Group | Calories |
|---|---|
| Girls 10–13 | 2,000–2,400 |
| Boys 10–13 | 2,200–2,800 |
| Girls 14–18 | 2,200–2,800 |
| Boys 14–18 in heavy training | 2,800–3,500 |
| Tournament weeks | up to 4,000–4,500 |

Schofield (1985) equations are standard but overestimate adolescent athlete
REE by 5–15% (Reale et al. 2020). Energy availability (kcal/kg FFM/day):
adolescents likely need higher than the adult 45 kcal/kg FFM threshold; <30 =
problematic LEA in adults but pediatric thresholds are not validated.

### Macronutrients

UEFA 2021 expert consensus (*BJSM* 55(8):416–442); AND/DC/ACSM 2016.

- **Carbohydrate (45–65% energy):** 3–5 g/kg/d light; 5–7 g/kg/d moderate (~1
  h); 6–10 g/kg/d high; 8–12 g/kg/d tournament. 6–8 g/kg the day before, 1–3
  g/kg 3–4 h pre-match, 30–60 g/h during, 1–1.5 g/kg/h first 4 h recovery
- **Protein:** 1.2–1.6 g/kg/d, up to 1.7 g/kg during heavy growth/training;
  distribute as ~0.25–0.3 g/kg per meal across 4–5 feedings; ~20 g (or 0.3
  g/kg) post-training; pre-sleep 30–40 g slow casein (Greek yogurt, milk,
  cottage cheese)
- **Fat:** 25–35% energy

### Hydration

NATA 2017 position (McDermott et al., *J Athl Train* 52(9):877–895):
- Pre-exercise 2–3 h before — 5–7 mL/kg
- During — 4–8 oz every 15–20 min (5 oz/20 min if ≤40 kg, 9 oz/20 min if >40
  kg per AAP/Bergeron)
- Post — 150% of body mass deficit within 4–6 h
- Goal: limit body mass loss <2%
- **WUT** (Weight, Urine color, Thirst) for monitoring

Sports drinks appropriate >60 min activity in heat. **AAP 2011 (Schneider &
Benjamin): energy drinks contraindicated in children/adolescents.**

### Match-day fueling

- **3–4 h pre-match:** 1–4 g/kg CHO, moderate protein, low fat/fiber
- **30–60 min pre-match:** 30–60 g CHO snack
- **Halftime:** 30–60 g CHO (Saltin 1973: glycogen drops 96 → 32 mmol/kg ww
  by halftime; Russell et al. 2012 — 6% CHO solution improved 2nd-half shot
  speed in academy youth)
- **Post-match:** 1.0–1.2 g/kg CHO + 0.3 g/kg protein within 30–60 min

### Iron deficiency

Affects ~40% of adolescent female athletes (Sim et al. 2019). RDA: boys 14–18
= 11 mg/d; girls 14–18 = 15–18 mg/d (athletes need ~+30–70%). Ferritin
thresholds (Clénin et al. Swiss SSM): adolescents 12–15 yr <20 μg/L =
deficient; 15–18 yr <30 μg/L low.

> **Refer for testing/treatment — AI must NOT prescribe iron.**

### Bone development

~90% of peak bone mass by age 18; 30% in the 3 years surrounding PHV (Bailey
1996). Calcium RDA 9–18 = 1,300 mg/d; vitamin D RDA 600 IU/d but athletes
commonly need 1,000–2,000 IU/d to maintain 25(OH)D ≥30 ng/mL.

> **Refer for vitamin D testing — do not blanket-supplement; toxicity >4,000
> IU/d chronic.**

### Supplementation in youth

- AAP (Gomez 2005, reaffirmed) condemns performance-enhancing supplements in
  youth; food-first
- Most youth do NOT need supplements
- Whey protein is unnecessary if dietary protein is adequate; not harmful
- **Creatine:** ISSN (Kreider et al. 2017) considers safe in adolescents with
  parental consent and supervision; AAP recommends against routine use under
  18; **AI default = "consult sports RD/MD"**
- **Caffeine and pre-workouts: AVOID in youth**; energy drinks contraindicated
- Up to 25% of supplements marketed as performance enhancers are contaminated
  with anabolic steroids; 20% with heavy metals; 11% with stimulants (AAP
  2016) — only NSF Certified for Sport, Informed Sport, or USP Verified if
  any supplement is used

### RED-S

Mountjoy et al. 2023 IOC consensus (*BJSM* 57(17):1073–1097): syndrome of
impaired physiological/psychological functioning from low energy availability —
bone, endocrine, menstrual, anabolism, growth, hematological, GI,
cardiovascular, immune, and psychological consequences; affects both males and
females. Female Athlete Triad is now framed as a subset.

**Critical refer-out triggers:**
- Unintentional weight loss
- Plateaued/declining growth velocity
- Delayed menarche (no period by 15) or secondary amenorrhea (≥3 months)
- Recurrent stress fractures
- Recurrent illness
- Restrictive eating, body checking, food preoccupation

> **AI must NOT manage RED-S or eating disorders; refer to RD (CSSD ideally) +
> MD + mental health.**

### Body composition tracking is hazardous in youth

DXA, BIA, skinfolds all have large measurement error vs. growth-related
variation; repeated measurement creates body focus that elevates
eating-disorder risk.

> **DO NOT** weigh youth athletes regularly or in front of peers, comment on
> appearance/weight, prescribe deficits, or label foods "good/bad/clean/dirty."
> **Track performance metrics** (sprint, jump, endurance, recovery) instead.

### Bilingual / cultural resources

- USDA MiPlato (myplate.gov/myplate-spanish)
- Salsa, Sabor y Salud (NLCI)
- Platillos Latinos Sabrosos y Saludables (NIH/NHLBI)
- ¡Podemos! (NHLBI)
- eatright.org en Español
- SNAP-Ed Spanish materials

Leverage cultural foods rather than replace them — arroz con pollo + frijoles
+ tortillas are exemplary pre-match fuel.

## 7. Recovery & sleep

### Sleep requirements

AASM consensus (Paruthi et al. 2016, *J Clin Sleep Med* 12(6):785–786,
AAP-endorsed):
- **Ages 6–12:** 9–12 h
- **Ages 13–18:** 8–10 h

Carskadon's "Perfect Storm" (2011, *Pediatr Clin N Am* 58:637–647) explains
the adolescent biological delayed sleep phase: slowed homeostatic sleep
pressure + circadian phase delay + heightened evening light sensitivity. AAP
(Owens et al. 2014, *Pediatrics* 134(3):642–649) recommends middle/high school
start times no earlier than 8:30 AM.

### Sleep–injury & performance evidence

- **Milewski et al. (2014, *J Pediatr Orthop* 34(2):129–133):** adolescents
  sleeping <8 h had 1.7× injury risk; 65% injured at <8 h vs. 31% at ≥8 h
- **Mah et al. 2011 (Stanford basketball, *Sleep* 34(7):943–950):** 5–7 weeks
  of sleep extension to ≥10 h in bed produced −0.7 sec on 282-foot sprint, +9%
  free-throw, +9.2% three-point
- Reilly & Edwards (2007) confirm circadian peak performance 16:00–20:00
- Watson 2017 ACSM/AMSSM: increased sleep is associated with improved
  performance, success, reduced injury and illness

### Active vs. passive recovery

Active recovery (50–60% HRmax, 10–30 min) accelerates lactate clearance but
Nédélec et al. 2013 (*Sports Med* 43:9–22) concluded it does NOT significantly
accelerate physical performance restoration in soccer. Practical: 10–15 min
light jog/cycle + dynamic mobility post-match; pool walk MD+1.

### Cold water immersion (10–15°C, 10–15 min)

Perceptual benefits and accelerated jump-recovery during congested fixtures
(Versey et al. 2013, *Sports Med* 43:1101–1130).

> **Critical caveat — Roberts et al. 2015 (*J Physiol* 593:4285–4301):**
> regular post-strength CWI attenuates long-term muscle and strength gains by
> blunting mTORC1 signaling and satellite cell activity for up to 48 h. **For
> youth: use CWI selectively (tournaments, congested fixtures), not
> habitually.**

Whole-body cryotherapy: insufficient pediatric evidence — **do not recommend
under 18.**

### Sleep hygiene for adolescents

Walsh et al. 2021 BJSM consensus; Hale & Guan 2015 (*Sleep Med Rev*
21:50–58) — screens adversely affected sleep in 90% of 67 studies:

- Consistent ±30 min sleep/wake times
- Bedroom 16–19°C, dark, quiet
- Screen sunset 60 min pre-bed
- No caffeine after 14:00 (half-life ~5 h)
- 30–60 min wind-down
- Warm shower 60–90 min pre-bed
- 30–40 g casein pre-bed supports overnight MPS

## 8. Mental performance & psychology

### Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan)

Three innate needs: autonomy, competence, relatedness. Need satisfaction →
autonomous motivation, well-being, persistence; need thwarting → controlled
motivation, ill-being, dropout.

Bartholomew, Ntoumanis, Ryan & Thøgersen-Ntoumani (2011, *PSPB* 37:1459):
thwarted needs predicted disordered eating, burnout, depression. Balaguer et
al. 2012: coach autonomy support → need satisfaction → well-being in youth
soccer.

**Practical autonomy-supportive coaching:** provide rationale, offer
meaningful choice, acknowledge feelings, minimize controlling language and
contingent rewards.

### Goal-setting

Burton; Locke & Latham (2002, *Am Psychol* 57:705–717): three types —
**outcome** (uncontrollable, anxiety-provoking), **performance** (personal
standards), **process** (controllable execution). Outcome goals damage youth
motivation because children can't dissociate self-worth from outcomes.

- **U10–U12:** single concrete process goal per session
- **U13–U15:** performance goals + strategy
- **U16–U18:** full SMART stack with athlete-led review

### Pressure and choking

Beilock & Carr 2001 (*JEP:Gen* 130:701) demonstrate two pathways: distraction
(working memory) and explicit monitoring (self-focus disrupts automatized
skill). Beilock specifically tested soccer dribbling. Adolescents <13
typically choke via distraction.

### Burnout

Smith's 1986 cognitive-affective model; Raedeke & Smith 2001 ABQ:
emotional/physical exhaustion + reduced accomplishment + sport devaluation.
Risks: high volume, year-round single sport, perfectionism, controlling
coaches, identity foreclosure.

**Prevention:** ≥3 months off/year, multi-sport sampling through ~13, weekly
wellness scoring, autonomy-supportive coaching.

### Coach–athlete–parent triad

Smoll & Smith CET research; Smoll, Smith, Barnett & Everett 1993 (*JAP*
78:602): 2.5-h preseason workshop produced 5% dropout vs. 26% control; higher
self-esteem; lower anxiety.

Pre-season parent meeting; **24-hour rule** (no game-talk parent↔coach for 24
h post-match); athlete-centered post-game conversation ("Did you have fun?
What did you learn?"). Sideline criticism is the worst-rated aspect of youth
sport (Holt et al. 2008).

### Failure resilience — 5 R's

Recognize, Release, Reset, Refocus, Repeat. Coach mistake-contingent
encouragement (Smoll & Smith) — immediate supportive comment + brief
technical instruction + future-focused next-rep. Errorful learning at 70–85%
success rate balances challenge and confidence.

## 9. Common myths and bad advice to reject

| Myth | Reality |
|---|---|
| "Strength training stunts growth" | Definitively wrong. NSCA 2009, 2014 International Consensus, IOC 2015, Malina 2006: no evidence. Growth-plate fractures have not been reported in supervised studies |
| "Younger is better for skill acquisition / 10,000 hours" | Macnamara, Hambrick & Oswald (2014, *Psych Sci* 25:1608) meta-analysis of 88 studies: deliberate practice explained only 18% of variance in sports performance, ~1% among elite |
| "Children are mini-adults" | Wrong on thermoregulation, glycolytic capacity, neural maturation, hormonal milieu, biomechanics, psychology, and recovery |
| "Early specialization produces better adult athletes" | Contrary to evidence. Multi-sport sampling correlates with greater elite achievement, lower burnout, lower injury, longer careers |

### Dangerous/outdated practices to reject

- Static stretching >30 sec/muscle pre-match (impairs power 4–8%)
- Long-distance running for "conditioning" of pre-PHV children
- "Cardio first, weights last" on same-session concurrent work
- Punitive conditioning (running as punishment) — undermines SDT
  need-satisfaction
- Year-round single-sport with no off-season
- Adult periodization templates without maturity adjustment
- Heading drills U11 and below (US Soccer banned)
- Skipping FIFA 11+ / 11+ Kids
- Maximal 1RM testing in unsupervised novice youth
- Depth jumps pre-PHV or without squat ≥1× BW
- Habitual post-training CWI (Roberts 2015 — blunts adaptation)
- Body composition tracking and weigh-ins for adolescents
- Energy drinks, pre-workouts, stimulants in any youth athlete
- Coach comments on athlete body or weight

## 10. Cultural & equity considerations

### Pay-to-play structure

US competitive club soccer typically costs **$2,000–$10,000/year**, with
ECNL/MLS NEXT levels reaching $5,000–$20,000 with travel. By contrast, La
Masia, La Fábrica, and DFB Stützpunkte are free; MLS academies are free but
recruit only from U13/U14, by which point pay-to-play has already filtered
the pool. National-team rosters historically under-represent Black, Latino,
and lower-income players relative to US demographics.

### Bilingual coaching

For the large US Hispanic/Latino youth soccer demographic: first-language
instruction supports concept transfer (Cummins 2000 bilingual education
research). Cultural foods are typically excellent youth-athlete fuel — arroz,
frijoles, tortillas, plátano, leche, queso fresco, pollo asado are
CHO/protein/calcium-rich; **leverage them rather than impose Western
alternatives.**

### Boys vs. girls

Female adolescents have **3–8× higher non-contact ACL risk** (Hewett 2005)
and ~1.5–2× higher concussion rate (Marar 2012). Mitigation requires
PEP/Knäkontroll/FIFA 11+ neuromuscular training. Match demands: similar
distances; lower absolute high-intensity outputs; menstrual-cycle and RED-S
considerations require female-specific awareness.

### Late vs. early developers — the underdog hypothesis

Gibbs et al. 2012; Cumming et al. 2018; Hill et al. 2023; Johnson, Farooq &
Whiteley 2017: late maturers who survive deselection often **outperform
early-maturing peers in adult professional play** because they had to develop
superior technique, tactics, and adaptability to compete despite physical
disadvantage. Academies should retain late maturers via bio-banding, "futures"
programs, and patient long-term tracking rather than cut on early physical
performance markers.

## 11. Knowledge boundary — what Gunny must NOT coach

Gunny may educate, screen, motivate, deliver standardized prevention programs
(FIFA 11+, FIFA 11+ Kids, PEP, Knee Control), enforce workload guidelines, and
reinforce safety policies.

**Gunny must NOT diagnose, prescribe, treat, or clear an athlete in any of the
following scenarios:**

1. **Suspected concussion** — recognize-and-remove only. Return-to-play is a
   MEDICAL DECISION (sports medicine MD, neurologist, ATC under physician
   oversight). Use CDC HEADS UP and the 5th/6th International Consensus
   framework (CRT5 for non-medical recognition)
2. **Acute injury** — audible pop, swelling, deformity, inability to bear
   weight, joint instability, suspected fracture/dislocation → emergency
   evaluation same-day
3. **Active rehabilitation of any injury** — refer to ATC, PT/DPT, or
   physician
4. **Suspected growth-plate (Salter-Harris) injury** — any peripubertal joint
   trauma must have X-ray; do not assume "sprain"
5. **Diagnosing growth-related conditions** (Sever's, Osgood-Schlatter, SLJS,
   Iselin's, apophyseal avulsion, OCD, SCFE, Perthes) — pediatrician /
   pediatric orthopedist / pediatric sports medicine physician
6. **Suspected RED-S, eating disorder, disordered eating, amenorrhea** —
   multidisciplinary referral (RD with ED expertise + MD + mental health).
   **Never coach through caloric restriction or weight goals with adolescents.
   Never recommend body comp tracking. Never label foods
   "good/bad/clean/dirty."**
7. **Iron, vitamin D, B12, or other suspected deficiency** — refer for
   testing; do not prescribe supplementation
8. **Any supplement question for athletes <16** other than basic multivitamin
   in restricted-diet contexts — refer to sports RD/MD
9. **Mental health concerns** — clinical depression, anxiety disorders,
   panic, OCD, PTSD, abuse, ADHD, learning disabilities, substance use,
   self-harm. Refer to qualified mental-health professional. **Suicidality:
   988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline; 911/ED if imminent risk. SafeSport:
   720-531-0340 / safesport.org for abuse-related concerns.**
10. **Persistent pain >1 week** in a youth athlete (DiFiori 2014 threshold)
    — medical evaluation
11. **Cardiac concerns** — chest pain, syncope, arrhythmia, seizure —
    emergency referral; never advise return-to-play
12. **Heat illness with neurologic signs** — emergency referral
13. **Pre-pubertal (Tanner I–II) athlete requesting performance-nutrition
    advice** — refer to sports RD
14. **Recurrent stress fractures or bone stress injuries** — sports medicine
    MD (also rule out RED-S)

### Gunny's appropriate scope

Prevention-program delivery, technique cueing, age-appropriate programming,
workload-rule enforcement, education, mental-skills training (imagery,
self-talk, focus, routines, breathing), motivational climate, goal-setting
frameworks, sleep/recovery education, parent communication, and
confidence-building. **Diagnosis, treatment, rehabilitation, and clearance
always belong to licensed clinicians.**
